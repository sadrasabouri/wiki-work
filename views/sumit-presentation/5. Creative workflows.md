# Creative Workflows for Presentation Generation

## The Workflows

### 1. Template + Fill
*[[T14-2026-05-05-2]] · Sumit, Sadra*

Define a template with `## Section` headings and `> fill:` prompts specifying what each slide should contain. Run it against the KB. The template encodes the argument structure; the fill prompts encode what evidence to pull.

Example: [[templates/sumit-lightning.md]] — each `> fill:` prompt specifies the audience, the source, and the claim to land.

---

### 2. FlashFill Convergence
*[[T15-2026-05-06]] · Sumit*

Start from the desired output shape. Generate a first draft. Revise it, then feed the revision back as a new input-output example. Repeat until the agent converges on the right framing.

Sumit used this for his Thursday pre-read: "each revision is another example, narrowing the hypothesis space." The skill is accumulating examples rather than specifying the methodology upfront.

---

### 3. Grounded Examples First
*[[T13-2026-05-05-1]] · Sumit*

Before building the structure, query the KB for the best concrete stories and examples. Let the examples drive the slide structure, not the other way around.

Sumit's explicit request: "3 concrete wiki examples for different audiences." The [[views/presentation-grounded-examples|grounded-examples presentation]] was generated this way — examples first, structure second. *[[T14-2026-05-05-2]]*

---

### 4. Audience-Aware Storytelling
*[[T14-2026-05-05-2]] · Sumit*

Hold the slide structure constant; vary the story selection per audience. The same KB contains stories relevant to engineers, managers, and executives — the skill picks different ones depending on `{{audience}}`. The template parameterizes the audience; the skill knows to match example specificity and vocabulary to it.

Example: [[templates/sumit-lightning.md]] uses `{{audience}}` to steer which failure stories to surface.

---

### 5. Argument-First / Claim-Then-Evidence
*[[T15-2026-05-06]] · Sumit*

Start with the thesis you want to land. Query the KB for evidence, counter-evidence, and tensions that support or complicate each claim. Build slides as claim → evidence → tension.

---

### 6. Self-Referential Anchoring
*[[T14-2026-05-05-2]] · Sumit*

Use the presentation itself as the first example of the thing it describes. Sumit committed in May 6 to opening with the raw-sources slide and explicitly revealing the trick: *"if I start the presentation by having this slide and saying the raw views can actually come from that, they will actually see they are being tricked and they will immediately get into what is happening here."*

Requires `self_referential: true` in the template frontmatter. *[[self-referential-kb]]*

---

### 8. Repeated Distillation and Mash-Up
*[[T15-2026-05-06]] · Sumit*

Sumit's signature workflow for his internal blog posts: produce multiple distillations, then have an agent (or ChatGPT, separately) **mash competing versions together**. The mashed-up output is often higher quality than any single distillation. He has 20+ document versions across V/M/N/T/S series — a real phenomenon, not just a quirk. Captured separately as [[version-sprawl]].

---

### 9. Debating Agents
*[[T15-2026-05-06]] · Sumit*

Multiple agents argue versions of the same artifact to surface the strongest synthesis. Pairs with mash-up: debate produces variants; mash-up reconciles them.

---

### 10. Topic-by-Topic / Side-by-Side Refinement
*[[T15-2026-05-06]] · Sumit*

For slide decks, group slides by topic and refine the group together rather than slide-by-slide. Sumit attributed this technique to a colleague and observed it generalizes: "you can do side by side or you can group them into different groups and then you can do outline by outline or topic by topic refinement."

---

### 11. Grounded Theory Summarization
*[[T15-2026-05-06]] · Sumit (credit: Nikhil Swami)*

Inductive coding → clustering → naming, applied to messy qualitative transcripts. Sumit named this as a "recent favorite" he learned from talking to people. Differs from generic summarization in that the categories *emerge* from the data.

---

### 12. Contradiction-Surfacing
*[[T9-2026-05-02-2]] · Sadra; [[T15-2026-05-06]] · Sumit*

Query the KB for tensions, unresolved questions, and *unspoken* questions. Use them as narrative hooks — "here's what we don't know yet" is often more compelling than "here's what we proved." Sumit explicitly asked for a "questions that were not asked" view in May 6.
