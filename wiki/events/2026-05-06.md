## Sumit's Document Generation Workflow

Sumit reverse-engineered the pre-read document by starting from the desired content outline and having an agent fill in each section — the document as a view with holes. Where sections were too short, he prompted the agent to merge paragraphs. [[T15-2026-05-06]]

The key analogy: **FlashFill**. FlashFill narrows a large space of possible transformation functions by accumulating input-output examples until it converges on the right one. Sumit proposed the same example-driven iterative approach for guiding an agent toward the right presentation output — each revision is another input-output example, narrowing the hypothesis space. He suggested applying this to Sadra's presentation as well.

This is [[views|document-as-view]] instantiated in practice: the structure is fixed (template), the agent fills holes (prompts), and the author converges through iteration rather than specification up front.

### The Right Level of Generality

Sumit deepened the FlashFill analogy in the evening session: when extracting a template from an example, the program (template) must be at the **right level of generality**. Too general and it produces "a 2000 word essay" (loses the voice); too specific and it just copies the example (no creativity). He recognized this as the same problem FlashFill solves with **program ranking** — the system returns not the first program consistent with the example, but the program most likely to generalize correctly to unseen inputs. Direct quote: *"if you come up with a generalization of an example that you have, which is too general, then you might get a very different newsletter, something that you might not like. Or if it is too specific, then you don't leave much room for creativity."*

Concrete moves Sumit found himself making:
- Group similar paragraphs *first*, then generalize at the group level
- Decide what to abstract: "talk about person X" → generalize to "talk about any person" sometimes, but not when X is Satya (the closing shout-out)
- Re-run the generalized template against the raw data, not just the original document

This is a [[skills|skill]] — distillation-via-template-extraction-at-the-right-level — and Sumit identified it as his "biggest find" of the day.

## Three-Layer Architecture, Made Explicit

Sumit's most consequential structural intervention: he insisted the directory layout reflect a **three-layer architecture** with `raw` / `wiki` / `views` as **peer top-level directories**. Previously `views/` lived under `wiki/`, which "defeats the entire thing — it makes it confusing because you have raw information, you have wiki, and then you have views." Three layers, three top-level folders.

This isn't cosmetic. It mirrors the [[information-knowledge-pipeline|information→knowledge→views pipeline]]: raw data is immutable; wiki is processed knowledge; views are purpose-specific renderings. Conflating layers in the file system would conflate them conceptually for anyone navigating the repo. The cleanup followed the same day.

## Souti: Emergence of Knowledge Across Networks

Souti contributed a research direction that Sumit called "the most interesting thing I have learned in a long time." There are ICLR papers (one published last year per Souti) modeling **emergence of knowledge** across networks: divergence/convergence metrics, link fragility (how easy is it to break a connection between two teams?), and predicting when two networks are *close to discovering a new idea* versus just resynthesizing old ones. ## Wiki vs. RAG: Compression as the Central Concern

Earlier in the day, Sadra presented the interpretability argument for graph-structured wikis over RAG: in a wiki, the dependency between two pieces of knowledge is a typed, traversable edge; in RAG, it's a cosine similarity score — opaque by design. Sumit acknowledged the argument but offered a different center of gravity: **data compression** is the more fundamental concern in RAG, not interpretability. A RAG index is a compressed representation of a corpus; the quality question is whether the compression preserves the semantics needed for downstream tasks. Sumit encouraged Sadra to develop the interpretability angle further. [[T15-2026-05-06]]

This reframes the wiki-vs-RAG distinction: the wiki's advantage isn't only interpretability, but also that its compression model (entity extraction + typed relations) is *declarative* — you can inspect, correct, and extend the compressed representation, whereas an embedding is not directly editable.
